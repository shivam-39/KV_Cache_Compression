document: The United States federal budget process begins each year when the President submits a budget request to Congress. This request outlines the administration's priorities for spending and revenue for the upcoming fiscal year, which runs from October 1 to September 30. The Office of Management and Budget, known as the OMB, coordinates the preparation of this request by working with all federal agencies to determine their funding needs. Each agency submits a budget justification that explains why certain levels of funding are necessary for their programs and operations. Congress then reviews the President's budget request through its own budget committees in both the House and Senate. These committees hold hearings where agency officials testify about their funding needs and priorities. The Congressional Budget Office, or CBO, provides independent analysis of the President's budget proposals and economic forecasts that differ from the administration's own projections. After the committees complete their review, Congress drafts its own budget resolution that sets overall spending and revenue targets. This resolution serves as a framework for the appropriations process that follows. The appropriations committees then draft twelve separate spending bills that fund different parts of the federal government. These bills must pass both chambers of Congress and be signed by the President to become law. If Congress cannot pass these bills before the start of the fiscal year, it must pass a continuing resolution to keep the government funded at existing levels. In some cases, Congress fails to pass either appropriations bills or a continuing resolution, resulting in a government shutdown where non-essential federal services are suspended. The debt ceiling is a separate but related issue that limits the total amount of money the federal government can borrow to meet its existing legal obligations. When the government spends more than it collects in revenue, it must borrow money by issuing Treasury securities. If borrowing reaches the debt ceiling, Congress must vote to raise or suspend the limit to avoid a default on government obligations. The budget process has become increasingly contentious in recent decades as political polarization has made bipartisan agreement more difficult. Continuing resolutions have become more common as Congress struggles to pass full appropriations bills on time. Government shutdowns, once rare, have occurred more frequently and lasted longer than in previous eras. Budget negotiations often become entangled with broader policy disputes unrelated to government funding, further complicating the process of passing spending legislation.
summary : The federal budget process starts with the President's annual request to Congress, coordinated by the OMB. Congress reviews it through budget committees, aided by CBO analysis, then drafts appropriations bills. If bills aren't passed before the fiscal year begins, continuing resolutions maintain funding. Failure to act causes government shutdowns. The debt ceiling separately limits borrowing. Increasing political polarization has made timely budget agreements more difficult in recent years.


document : Climate change adaptation in coastal cities has become one of the most pressing urban planning challenges of the twenty-first century. As sea levels continue to rise due to the thermal expansion of oceans and the melting of polar ice sheets, cities located along coastlines face increasing risks from flooding, storm surges, and erosion. Urban planners and engineers are developing a range of strategies to address these threats, from large-scale infrastructure projects to nature-based solutions that work with natural ecosystems rather than against them. Hard infrastructure approaches include the construction of seawalls, flood barriers, and elevated roadways that physically block or redirect floodwaters. The Netherlands has been a leader in this approach, having developed an extensive system of dikes, storm surge barriers, and water management infrastructure over centuries of living below sea level. New York City began investing heavily in coastal protection following the devastating flooding caused by Hurricane Sandy in 2012, which resulted in billions of dollars in damage and exposed significant vulnerabilities in the city's infrastructure. Nature-based solutions offer an alternative or complementary approach to hard infrastructure. Restoring or creating coastal wetlands, mangrove forests, and oyster reefs can provide natural buffers against wave action and storm surges while also delivering ecological benefits such as improved water quality and habitat for marine species. These approaches are often more cost-effective over the long term than hard infrastructure and can adapt more flexibly to changing conditions. Managed retreat is a more controversial strategy that involves relocating communities and infrastructure away from the most vulnerable coastal areas rather than attempting to protect them in place. While this approach eliminates flood risk for relocated residents, it raises difficult questions about property rights, community identity, and equitable treatment of lower-income residents who may have fewer resources to relocate. Cities in low-lying developing nations face particular challenges because they often lack the financial resources and technical capacity to implement large-scale adaptation measures. Small island nations such as the Maldives and Tuvalu face existential threats from rising seas and have called for more ambitious global action to reduce greenhouse gas emissions. Urban heat islands compound the challenges faced by coastal cities, as densely built environments trap heat and increase energy demand for cooling during summer months.
summary : Coastal cities face growing threats from rising sea levels, prompting diverse adaptation strategies. Hard infrastructure like seawalls, exemplified by the Netherlands and post-Sandy New York, provides direct protection. Nature-based solutions such as wetland restoration offer cost-effective, flexible alternatives. Managed retreat relocates vulnerable communities but raises equity concerns. Developing nations and small island states face the greatest challenges with the fewest resources, while urban heat islands add further strain.


document: The development of messenger RNA vaccines represented a significant turning point in the history of vaccinology, bringing to fruition decades of research that had long struggled to translate laboratory promise into clinical reality. The fundamental concept behind mRNA vaccines is relatively straightforward: instead of introducing a weakened or inactivated pathogen into the body, or even a protein from that pathogen, the vaccine delivers genetic instructions that tell the body's own cells to produce a specific protein. The immune system then learns to recognize and respond to that protein, preparing it to fight the actual pathogen if encountered in the future. The scientific foundations for this approach were laid in the early 1990s when researchers demonstrated that synthetic mRNA could be delivered into cells and used to produce proteins. However, early mRNA molecules were unstable and triggered strong inflammatory responses in the body that limited their therapeutic potential. Katalin Karikó and Drew Weissman made a critical breakthrough in 2005 when they discovered that modifying certain chemical components of mRNA could dramatically reduce these inflammatory responses while maintaining the molecule's ability to direct protein production. This discovery, which would eventually earn them the Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine in 2023, removed a major obstacle to the development of mRNA therapeutics. The lipid nanoparticle delivery system presented another significant challenge. Raw mRNA cannot easily enter cells on its own and degrades rapidly in the body. Researchers developed tiny fat-based particles that could encapsulate and protect mRNA molecules while facilitating their entry into cells. Perfecting these delivery systems required years of additional research and represented a crucial enabling technology for the eventual vaccines. When the COVID-19 pandemic began in early 2020, companies including Moderna and BioNTech were already working on mRNA vaccine platforms for other diseases including influenza and cancer. The genomic sequence of the SARS-CoV-2 virus was published in January 2020, allowing these companies to rapidly design mRNA sequences coding for the viral spike protein. The unprecedented speed of vaccine development was made possible not only by the existing platform technology but also by massive government funding, streamlined regulatory processes, and the conduct of clinical trial phases in parallel rather than sequentially.

Though the concept emerged in the early 1990s, progress was blocked by mRNA instability and inflammation. A 2005 breakthrough by Karikó and Weissman, later recognized with a Nobel Prize, resolved this. Lipid nanoparticles solved the delivery problem. When COVID-19 emerged, existing mRNA platforms, government funding, and parallel trial design enabled record-speed vaccine development.
